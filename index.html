As COVID-19 Continues To Spread Across US, Census Completion Rates Lag

Photo credit: U.S. Census Bureau

By Ana Lucia Murillo
Published May 16, 2020 8:00 PM

At the same time as COVID-19 is wreaking havoc on communities across the U.S., the national decennial census is happening. Originally set to end in July, the due date for the census has been extended to the end of October due to the coronavirus pandemic.

In some counties there appears to be a small correlation between higher rates of coronavirus infections and lower rates of census completion. In the 10 counties studied, those with the highest rates of coronavirus among their population also had the lowest census completion rates.

Using data from the New York Times and the U.S. Census Bureau, I studied the five most populous suburban counties in the metropolitan area of Washington, D.C. and New York City. Originally I was going to chart the cities themselves, but the New York Times data was not broken down by county for New York City.

Westchester County outside NYC has had about 32 cases per 1,000 residents, and has a census return rate of about 59 percent so far. Meanwhile, Fairfax County, a suburb of Washington, D.C., has had about six cases of coronavirus per 1,000 residents, and has a census return rate of about 73 percent so far.

The completion rate is currently seven percent below the completion rate ten years ago. Although there are still six months left to collect census data, in-person census taking is now on hold due to coronavirus.

There were already concerns that people wouldn’t fill out the census due to the Trump administration’s efforts to put a citizenship question on the questionnaire. The question was blocked by the Supreme Court, but not before creating a lot of fear in undocumented communities.

“I don’t think anyone was prepared to conduct the census in the middle of a pandemic,” said Jay Goldfinger, legislative and communications director for state assembly member Mathylde Frontus of Coney Island, a part of Brooklyn where census completion rates are well below 50 percent. “What [the NYC census] did was the result of years of good planning and now a good chunk of that has gone out the window.”

U.S. Census data is used to decide how federal funding gets split up and how many House seats states get. Census data determines federal funding for things like schools, hospitals, and roads. Businesses also use census data to decide where to invest in things like factories and new stores, which bring jobs.

The fewer people who respond in a given area, the more likely that area is to get short-changed in the future and struggle to get the infrastructure and funding to help with an economic recovery from the effects of the pandemic.
